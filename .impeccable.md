## Design Context

### Users
Ambitious, goal-oriented people — fresh graduates, career changers, and working professionals actively pursuing specific career goals. They use Career OS with purpose: to make a concrete decision or take a concrete action, not to browse passively. They are intelligent, impatient with fluff, and reward interfaces that respect their time and give them real data. Emotional state: driven, focused, slightly impatient — they want clarity fast.

Secondary audience: employers and hiring managers who are busy, pragmatic, and comparing candidates. They need density and filtering, not marketing copy.

### Brand Personality
Professional and sharp. Three words: **precise, driven, sharp**. Think LinkedIn-meets-design-studio: serious but not cold, structured but not corporate-stiff. Feels like a tool built by people who take careers seriously. No hype, no motivational filler — just clear information and actionable tools.

Anti-reference: avoid anything that looks like a generic job board, an app made in Webflow's template gallery, or anything with glowing orbs and purple gradients.

### Aesthetic Direction
Dark mode. Deep navy-black surfaces (`oklch(0.13 0.012 258)`) — not pure black, tinted toward cool blue to feel focused rather than void. Amber/gold accent (`oklch(0.82 0.14 72)`) for CTAs, highlights, and key data points — conveys ambition and achievement, unexpected for a career product, and highly memorable.

Typography: **Bricolage Grotesque** for headings and display — geometric variable font with subtle letterform character, feels designed rather than neutral, holds authority on dark backgrounds. **Geist Sans** for body and UI text — already loaded with Next.js, efficient and slightly technical, fits the "productivity tool" register. Tabular-nums for all salary and numeric data.

One thing users will remember: amber numbers on dark surfaces — salary benchmarks, career stats, and progress metrics that feel like a trading terminal.

### Design Principles
1. **Data first, decoration second** — every visual element should help the user make a decision or understand their position. Remove anything that doesn't do this.
2. **Density with breathing room** — present more information than a typical career app, but give important things space to land. Not sparse, not cluttered — editorial.
3. **Earned hierarchy** — not everything is a card. Use contrast, weight, and spacing to signal importance. Reserve bold accents for what actually matters.
4. **Purposeful dark** — the dark theme is not decorative. It creates focus, reduces visual noise for people working through complex decisions, and signals this is a serious tool.
5. **No false warmth** — no rounded corners on everything, no motivational micro-copy ("You're doing great!"), no emoji in interface labels. Warmth comes from good information and honest feedback, not decorative cheerfulness.

### Component Notes
- **Navigation**: Left sidebar on desktop (not top nav — signals productivity app, not marketing site). Compact icon + label, amber dot for active state.
- **Cards**: Use sparingly. Profile stats and salary data use inline rows, not cards. Job listings and candidate profiles are cards — but flat-bordered, no drop shadow.
- **Data tables**: `font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums` everywhere. Salary in amber. Role titles in primary text weight.
- **Buttons**: Primary = amber fill, dark text. Secondary = outlined, border-strong. Ghost = text only with hover state. Never more than one primary CTA visible at once.
- **Forms (onboarding)**: Full-screen stepped layout — one decision per screen. Progress bar in amber.
- **Empty states**: Show what the user will get once filled — wireframe-style preview of the feature, not a generic "nothing here" message.
